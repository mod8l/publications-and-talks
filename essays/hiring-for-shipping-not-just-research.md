# Hiring for Shipping, Not Just Research

If I had to hire one ML engineer for a startup, I would not choose the candidate
with the most citations. I would choose the one who has shipped something real,
felt it break at 2 a.m., and learned from the scars.

Research excellence and production excellence are related but not the same.
Research asks whether something is possible. Production asks whether it will
keep working when the data shifts, the team changes, and the customer expects
results today. Most startups need the second skill far more than the first.

## The research trap

It is easy to be seduced by credentials. A PhD from a top lab, a paper at a
flagship conference, a GitHub repository with thousands of stars. These are
signals of ability, but they are not signals of fit for a startup that needs to
ship.

I have hired researchers who struggled in production because they were not used
to ambiguity. They wanted clean datasets, defined metrics, and time to iterate.
Production gave them messy logs, shifting requirements, and a Slack message
asking why latency had doubled since lunch.

That is not a criticism of researchers. It is a mismatch of context. Some of
the best ML engineers I know came from research and learned to ship. But the
transition requires a mindset shift: from optimizing a score to owning an
outcome.

## What shipping looks like

When I interview ML engineers, I look for evidence of ownership across the full
lifecycle. Can they describe how a model they built was deployed? Can they
explain the monitoring they set up, the failures they saw, and the changes they
made as a result?

I ask about a time a model degraded in production. If the candidate says "that
never happened," I do not believe them. If they say "it happened and here is
what we learned," I lean in. Production ML is the art of managing decay. The
best engineers are comfortable with that reality.

I also look for practical habits. Do they version data and models together? Do
they write tests for feature engineering? Do they know what a confusion matrix
looks like for last week's traffic, not just the test set? These details
separate someone who can run a notebook from someone who can run a system.

## Team balance matters more than individual brilliance

A startup does not need a roster of unicorns. It needs a team whose skills
overlap enough to cover the gaps. I like to pair a strong scientist with a
strong engineer and make them jointly own a product outcome. The scientist
pushes accuracy. The engineer pushes reliability. Together they learn the
language of the other side.

I also make room for generalists. The person who can clean data, train a
baseline, wrap it in an API, and debug a Kubernetes pod at midnight is rare and
valuable. They may not be the deepest expert in any one area, but in a startup,
breadth is often the superpower.

## Interview signals

My favorite interview question is simple: "Tell me about something you shipped
that had a user." The answer tells me almost everything. I listen for who else
was involved, what trade-offs they made, what went wrong, and whether they
still care about the outcome.

I am wary of candidates who describe projects only in terms of metrics and
architectures. I am drawn to candidates who talk about users, latency, cost,
maintenance, and team dynamics. Those are the people who understand that
shipping is a team sport with business consequences.

I also pay attention to how candidates talk about failure. Do they blame the
data, the model, the platform, or do they describe what they changed? The
willingness to own a broken pipeline and fix it is worth more than any
leaderboard score.

Finally, I look for curiosity about the user. The best shipping engineers can
explain who uses their model, what decision it supports, and what happens when
it is wrong. They do not hide behind AUROC. They translate model behavior into
human outcomes.

## The final test

Hiring for shipping means hiring for accountability. The right candidate does
not just want to build models. They want to own the loop: idea, data, training,
deployment, monitoring, and iteration. They know that the finish line is not a
leaderboard. It is a customer who keeps coming back because the system works.

That is the kind of ML engineer who helps a startup survive the valley between
demo and product.
